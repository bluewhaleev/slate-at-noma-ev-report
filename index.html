02 / An Honest Read of the Data

What the survey is really telling us.

Signal: charging reads as a retention lever

Every respondent would use on-site chargers weekly or more, and 75% say it affects whether they keep living here. In a dense, transit-rich part of DC, residents treat charging as a reason to stay, which makes it a leasing and renewal lever.

Signal: keep it right-sized and fair

The sample is small (8 responses), and one resident flagged cost fairness. Treat this as justification for a modest first phase, and get the operating model right: a user-funded price so non-EV residents never subsidize the chargers.

06 / Recommendation

A measured path forward.

Proposed next step

Start with a right-sized, user-funded 4-port pilot.

The signal is early but consistent, and the economics point one direction. The next move is to bring ownership in, lock a real quote, and settle the operating model before anything gets installed.

I.

Share With Ownership

Put this report in front of ownership and have Blue Whale EV walk through the findings, the demand case, and the recommended first phase.

II.

Lock Updated Quote

Confirm charger type, count, and site design so hardware, install, and operating assumptions reflect the final plan instead of calculator defaults.

III.

Confirm Operating Model

Set a user-funded price so non-EV residents don't subsidize the chargers, and define access control. That answers the fairness point residents raised.

IV.

Begin Install Process

Once the contract is signed, start permitting, Pepco coordination, equipment ordering, and installation scheduling.
